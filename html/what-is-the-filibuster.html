What is the filibuster?

An engine of compromise or a legislative roadblock, depending on whom you ask

IT TOOK MORE than three weeks after America’s new Congress convened at the start of January for Charles Schumer and Mitch McConnell, the Senate majority and minority leaders, to agree on an organising resolution, which lays out committee assignments and other aspects of the chamber’s functioning. The sticking-point was Mr McConnell’s demand that Mr Schumer promise not to abolish the filibuster, as many on the left have urged. Senate Democrats had no immediate plans for its abolition, but neither did they want to restrict their options. Ultimately, the question was moot: getting rid of the filibuster would require the votes of all 50 Democrats (plus Kamala Harris, who as vice-president is also president of the Senate, and casts tie-breaking votes), and two of them—Joe Manchin of West Virginia and Kyrsten Sinema of Arizona—said publicly that they opposed abolishing it. What is the filibuster, and why has a congressional quirk provoked such strong passions?

Loosely, a filibuster refers to an attempt to block or delay legislation. The most well-known way to do this is to stand on the Senate floor and fulminate at length, as Jimmy Stewart did in “Mr Smith Goes to Washington”, a film released in 1939 about a senator taking on the system. Yet this tactic—the “talking filibuster”—is rarely deployed. The modern filibuster is a byproduct of the Senate’s cloture rule, passed in 1917, which required two-thirds (now three-fifths, after a change in 1975) of the Senate to agree to end debate on a bill and allow it to proceed to a vote on the Senate floor.

But a bill does not need to be under active discussion to be still technically under debate, which can continue even while the Senate is discussing something else entirely, or when the lights on the Senate floor are off and senators are eating Thanksgiving dinner in their home states. If at least 41 senators make it known that they plan to oppose a cloture motion, the bill at issue is denied the requisite three-fifths, and it cannot come up for a vote. In this situation, Senate leaders, who have discretion over when and whether to schedule cloture votes, often decide not to bother. The minority party can therefore kill legislation without casting a single vote. Historically, the cloture rule was invoked only infrequently. In the present era of polarisation, the number of cloture votes—a proxy for the total number of filibusters—has risen dramatically, from around 50 per year at the turn of the millennium to more than 250 in the Congress just ended (see chart).

Mr Manchin, like Ms Sinema and other defenders of the filibuster, argues that the manoeuvre encourages compromise and bipartisanship by forcing concessions from the majority. Progessive Democrats say that the filibuster makes passing legislation functionally impossible and agitate for its removal. Others note that the founders opposed a supermajority requirement, that the modern filibuster impedes rather than encourages debate and compromise, and that passing legislation is far more difficult in America than in other advanced democracies. Barack Obama has called it a “Jim Crow relic”—and indeed, in the mid-20th century, segregationist senators often filibustered civil-rights legislation. President Joe Biden, who suggested on the campaign trail he would be open to getting rid of the filibuster, has been more circumspect since taking office.

And the filibuster is not an all-or-nothing proposition. Senators can institute a rule requiring that a filibustering senator actually speak, ensuring that debate is not purely theoretical. They can also carve out exceptions (the process of overriding a rule with a simple majority is known as the “nuclear option”), as the Senate has done many times: bills on which a cloture vote is not required include proposals to close military bases and ratify trade agreements. In 2013, the Senate, then controlled by the Democrats, changed the rules to require just a simple majority for most executive-branch and federal judicial nominations. In 2017, Republicans did the same for the Supreme Court nomination of Neil Gorsuch, who was ultimately confirmed with 54 votes. For now, thanks to Mr Manchin and Ms Sinema, the filibuster is here to stay. Its opponents will not be able to eliminate it completely. But they may be able to water it down.
