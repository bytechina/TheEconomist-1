Why abortion rights are under threat in America

The Supreme Court’s conservative majority seems poised to narrow Roe v Wade

FIVE YEARS ago, on the eve of the 2016 presidential election, Donald Trump pledged to appoint Supreme Court justices who would “automatically” overturn Roe v Wade, the ruling from 1973 that established a constitutional right to abortion. Although nothing is automatic at the Supreme Court, Mr Trump’s promise seems close to fruition. During his term in office Mr Trump transformed the tribunal by appointing three new justices, giving conservatives a 6-3 majority. Now the court is considering the constitutionality of a Mississippi abortion ban passed in 2018—a case that tees up Roe’s reversal.

The oral arguments in Dobbs v Jackson Women’s Health Organisation began on December 1st; the case will probably be decided by June 2022. It comes amid a torrent of state regulations to undermine access to abortion. This year at least 70 restrictions have taken effect in 14 states. In May one of the most onerous rules introduced this year—a near-total ban at six weeks’ gestation, before many women are aware they are pregnant—became law in Texas (though it is still possible the Supreme Court will strike it down). The law in question in Dobbs, the Gestational Age Act, sounds mild by comparison: it bans abortion after 15 weeks of pregnancy. But it gives the Supreme Court’s conservative majority an opportunity to lay waste to the abortion right recognised nearly a half-century ago.

In Roe, the court’s justices sought to balance a woman’s right to privacy concerning her pregnancy with a state’s interest in protecting potential life. Justice Harry Blackmun and his colleagues held that viability—the stage when a fetus can survive outside the womb, then about 27 weeks into a pregnancy but now around 23-24 weeks—is the point when states may ban abortion. When the Supreme Court heard another case in 1992, Planned Parenthood v Casey, supporters of abortion choice braced for the worst. The constitutional right to abortion seemed on the verge of nullifcation. But in a surprise ruling, the Casey court largely stuck with Roe. It permitted some regulations (like a 24-hour waiting period before an abortion) but said that placing “substantial obstacles” to the procedure (such as requiring a woman to inform her spouse before having a termination), as well as outright pre-viability bans, were unconstitutional.

There is little hope of a repeat surprise. Mississippi’s law banning abortion before viability is, as both the district court and the highly conservative Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals saw, blatantly unconstitutional under Roe and Casey. The question for the Supreme Court, then, is whether all pre-viability abortion bans really are unconstitutional—in other words, whether those decades-old precedents should stand. Where the public stands is tricky to divine. Polls consistently show that a majority of Americans would object to the court overturning Roe, but others show that a majority wants limits on abortions that Roe does not currently permit. It seems the six conservative justices have the will to abandon or radically re-envision Roe, but do they have a way?

The most straightforward—if politically fraught—approach would be to overrule Roe and Casey and erase a right that was, in the eyes of its opponents, fabricated rather than discovered. That would be a highly unusual step for the court to take. A somewhat less radical tack would render Roe useless rather than explicitly overrule it. The court could decide that viability should no longer be the decisive factor, and allow states to ban abortion much earlier in pregnancy. Or it could declare that banning abortion after 15 weeks is not a substantial obstacle since most women who end their pregnancies choose to do so earlier on. Under any of these scenarios, more liberal-minded states would remain free to protect abortion choice while conservatives ones would have a freer hand to foreclose it—meaning that a woman’s reproductive rights would be contingent on where she lives. However the justices rule, the decision will inflame political passions, not least because it will land in the heat of the mid-term election campaigns.
