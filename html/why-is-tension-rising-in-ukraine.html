Why is tension rising in Ukraine?

It is a reminder that Vladimir Putin calls the shots

RUSSIAN TROOPS are once again massing along Ukraine’s eastern border, as well as in Crimea, raising fears of a return to war between the Ukrainian government and two Moscow-backed breakaway republics, of a kind last seen six years ago. When Russian soldiers moved into eastern Ukraine to support separatists there in 2014, the fighting forced an overwhelmed Ukrainian government to the negotiating table. In 2015 the two sides agreed to an internationally brokered ceasefire and a politically unrealistic deal for “decentralised” government known as the Minsk agreements. But since then they have squabbled over the deal’s terms, and routinely accuse each other of violating the truce. Still, the conflict has remained on a low simmer. Recently, however, violence in eastern Ukraine has increased sharply (the killing of a Ukrainian soldier last week was the 28th this year) and the Kremlin has beat its war drum louder. Why?

Start with events in February 2014, when protesters in Kyiv, Ukraine’s capital, toppled a pro-Russian autocrat—the country’s second revolution against a corrupt leader in a decade. Vladimir Putin, Russia’s autocratic president, has long been wary of democratic movements in his back yard, especially in Ukraine, which for centuries had been an integral part of the Russian and Soviet empires. The Maidan revolution, as it was known, galvanised a nation against Russian influence and set Kyiv on a firmly pro-Western course. Yet the tumult left the Ukrainian state—hollowed out by decades of graft—distracted and unable to defend itself. Sensing this weakness, Russian forces invaded and annexed Crimea, in the south. The Kremlin then stoked insurgencies in the eastern region of Donbas. Powerful propaganda fomented fears that Russian-speakers in those territories would be targeted by an allegedly “fascist” government in Kyiv.

More than 13,000 people have been killed and another 1.5m internally displaced by the fighting in eastern Ukraine. Crucially for the Kremlin, the situation in the Donbas has kept Ukraine politically unstable and Russia engaged in the country’s affairs. For Mr Putin the gambit initially paid off. His approval ratings soared at home as he cut the image of a protector of Russian-speakers and a bulwark against an encroaching West.

Today his position looks less assured. The Russian economy is stagnating, partly under the weight of Western sanctions, and Mr Putin has faced protests over the jailing of his chief political opponent, Alexei Navalny. An entire generation of Russians has come of age knowing only his rigid rule. Meanwhile, the new American president, Joe Biden, supports Ukraine’s embrace of the West and has been more willing to confront Russia. Once hopeful that he could make peace with Mr Putin, Volodymyr Zelensky, Ukraine’s president, has instead turned against his main political ally in Ukraine, a tycoon named Viktor Medvedchuk who peddles Russian propaganda. These political shifts are probably what sparked Mr Putin into action again, eager to show that Russia is still a force to be reckoned with.

As he did seven years earlier, Mr Putin is forcing the West to play by his rules of diplomacy. In March Mr Biden refused to hold talks with his Russian counterpart. The build-up of troops on Ukraine’s border has now spurred the American president to propose a summit. But Mr Biden seems keener than his predecessor was to punish Mr Putin’s poor behaviour, applying new sanctions against Russia. Ukrainians, for their part, are pleased to have a strong ally again in the White House, but they also worry that they will be trapped between great powers and their country’s fate will be decided for them, as has often happened to it in the past. So long as Ukraine remains wedged between a revanchist Russia and the West, any hope of a lasting peace seems elusive.
