Why the CDU/CSU’s Armin Laschet is floundering in Germany’s election

His lacklustre showing could mean that Angela Merkel’s party loses office

IT WAS SUPPOSED to be easy for Armin Laschet. The candidate of the conservative Christian Democrats (CDU) and their Bavarian allies, the Christian Social Union (CSU), to succeed Angela Merkel in Germany’s chancellery had hoped to coast to victory in this month’s election. Instead, Mr Laschet finds himself leading a campaign that has disintegrated more quickly than even his biggest foes can have hoped.

As the candidate for a grouping that has led Germany’s government for a decade-and-a-half, Mr Laschet is caught in an awkward position: pledging change, but not so much as to disturb a conservative electorate broadly content with the direction of the country. He vows to usher in a “decade of modernisation” on climate protection, digitisation and more. He promises to ease legal and bureaucratic hurdles to building infrastructure, and to return Germany’s fiscal policy to balanced-budget orthodoxy after a pandemic-inspired splurge. But he has struggled to find a consistent explanation for why he, and not one of his rivals, deserves to run the country. According to polls, many more Germans would rather give the job to Olaf Scholz of the centre-left Social Democrats.

Mr Laschet sits on the left of his party. He was part of the “Pizza connection”, a discussion club in Bonn between the CDU and the Greens in the 1990s, considered eccentric at the time. As an integration minister (Germany’s first) in North Rhine-Westphalia (NRW), the large, western state he has run since 2017, he earned the nickname “Turkish Armin” from some of his party’s less enlightened colleagues for making overtures to Germans with an “immigration background”. (He also backed Mrs Merkel’s controversial refugee policy in 2015-16.) But more recently, his insistence that Germany’s climate ambitions must not be pursued at the expense of its industrial base has alienated some progressives. During abortive coalition talks with the Greens after the 2017 federal election Mr Laschet led his party’s negotiations on climate and industry; everyone found the experience bruising.

As a moderate inside the centre-right CDU, Mr Laschet is sometimes compared to the outgoing chancellor. The similarities only go so far. Mrs Merkel is an east German Protestant, raised in a dictatorship, who always remained oddly aloof from the party she led for 18 years (she resigned its leadership in 2018). Mr Laschet, who was born, brought up, and still lives, in the western town of Aachen, near the Dutch and Belgian borders, joined the CDU as a teenager. He slowly worked his way up, serving in state politics as well as in the German and European parliaments. He is a devoted Catholic, as are some of his closest advisers. Where Mrs Merkel is cool and collected, Mr Laschet manifests the joviality with which the Rhineland is associated. He is an enthusiastic participant in its annual Karneval (carnival) rituals. Mrs Merkel detests the spectacle.

Perhaps more important, Mr Laschet shows few signs of Mrs Merkel’s trademark calm and poise. His interventions during the pandemic were erratic and sometimes error-strewn. After devastating floods hit parts of NRW in July, Mr Laschet was filmed chortling in the background as Germany’s president intoned solemnly to television cameras. However unfairly, that seems to have stoked an already widespread sense that Mr Laschet is an unserious candidate ill-suited to the highest office. His personal approval ratings are catastrophically bad, which has helped drive national support for the CDU/CSU to some of their lowest-ever levels.

His defenders reply that Mr Laschet has been serially underestimated. In 2017 he overcame a 14-percentage-point deficit two months before voting day to eject the Social Democrats from the leadership of NRW, transforming himself into one of the CDU’s biggest beasts. In January he beat Friedrich Merz, a favourite of the CDU’s conservative base, to the leadership of the party. A few months later he saw off a challenge from the more popular Markus Söder, leader of the CSU, to become the two parties’ joint chancellor-candidate (to the lasting regret of many in both parties). Mr Laschet still has a few weeks to show that he has one more upset in him. But should he lead his party out of government entirely, the reckoning will be biblical.
