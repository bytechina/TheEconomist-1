A year since the death of George Floyd, Americans remain gloomy about race relations

The murder conviction of the cop who killed him does not seem to have improved relations between the police and non-whites

Editor’s note: Twelve months on from the killing of George Floyd, The Economist is publishing a series of articles, films, podcasts, data visualisations and guest contributions on the theme of race in America. To see them visit our hub

MAY 25TH will mark one year since George Floyd, an unarmed black man, was murdered by a police officer in Minneapolis, Minnesota. Mr Floyd’s death sparked months of Black Lives Matter protests across America and around the world. It ignited a campaign to reallocate funds from police departments—“defund the police”—to other public services and set off an election-year battle over “law and order” in America’s cities.

The deaths of black people at the hands of police also rocked communities in Kenosha, Wisconsin; Louisville, Kentucky; Atlanta, Georgia; and other places in 2020. According to data collected by Mapping Police Violence, a website, 1,127 people were killed by the police last year. Officers were three times more likely to kill black Americans than white ones. Tensions on the streets last summer—with the division exploited by a divisive President Donald Trump—were more heightened than they had been for years. The scenes were reminiscent of 2014 after Michael Brown, another unarmed black man, was shot dead by a police officer in Ferguson, Missouri.

Over the past year, during which Joe Biden replaced Mr Trump in the White House, Americans think race relations have improved only marginally. According to survey data from YouGov, collected on behalf of The Economist, the share of Americans who believe that race relations in America are “generally bad” fell by roughly seven percentage points, from 72% in May 2020 (in the immediate aftermath of Mr Floyd's death) to 65% in April.

Opinions on the state of relations between the police and non-whites are equally downcast. Roughly 59% of adults say they are “bad”, nearly equal to the 62% who said the same a year ago. YouGov also asked whether they thought relations had improved since Mr Floyd’s murder. Just one in ten of the adults surveyed on May 15th thought so; nearly two-fifths felt they have worsened; and half said they were unchanged. Although the police officer who killed George Floyd, Derek Chauvin, was convicted of murder last month, it is clear that the majority of Americans are still gloomy about the country's racial unity.
