How does a leadership challenge work in Britain’s Conservative Party?

Boris Johnson could be subjected to one imminently

BORIS JOHNSON’S job is on the line. In May 2020, with Britain in strict lockdown, Downing Street officials held a drinks party in the prime minister’s garden. Mr Johnson attended, though he claims he believed it was a “work event”. He has apologised, and insists on waiting for the outcome of an inquiry being carried out by a civil servant, but that has not stopped many Conservative MPs turning against him. On January 19th David Davis, a former minister, stood up in Parliament and demanded of Mr Johnson: “In the name of God, go.” But how would Conservative MPs go about removing Mr Johnson? And who would choose his successor?

Under the Conservative Party’s rules, a confidence vote is triggered when 15% of its MPs send letters of no confidence to the chair of the 1922 Committee, which represents Tory backbenchers. The party has 359 MPs, so 54 have to write in. All Conservative MPs may vote in the ballot, with the result dictated by a simple majority. This can happen quickly: in December 2018, the confidence vote in Theresa May came on the very same day it was triggered. If Mr Johnson wins, he stays on as leader. As an added bonus, another challenge cannot be launched for a year. If Mr Johnson loses, he would no longer be Conservative leader (though would remain as prime minister until a successor was chosen). Selecting a new leader would then involve two processes. First, the candidates must win the votes of fellow MPs. In consecutive rounds of voting, the candidate in last place is eliminated each time, until only two remain. These two names are then put forward to the estimated 180,000 members of the Conservative Party. They are the happy few who would have a direct say on who becomes Britain’s next prime minister.

Britain, then, can change its prime minister without the bother of a general election. It is relatively common. After Theresa May resigned between elections, Mr Johnson saw off other Tories to succeed her, as Mrs May did after David Cameron quit. (Neither Mr Cameron nor Mrs May lost a party confidence vote.) The leader who can command a majority in the House of Commons is asked by the queen to form a government, however they ended up in that position.

Mr Johnson might discern some light in the gloom. It is one thing to muster 54 MPs fed up with the prime minister. But it is quite another to persuade 180 to vote against him. Another Conservative prime minister would face a difficult period of rising inflation and rampant covid-19. His prospects and those of his party may look better in a year. But MPs may not want to find out. Those who are supportive of the prime minister in public may express a different view in the privacy of a voting booth.
