Do by-election defeats spell the end of British governments?

They are often just a protest vote—but too many upsets may spell trouble

NORTH SHROPSHIRE, a constituency in the English West Midlands, is dyed the deepest blue. Since its creation in 1832 it has always returned a Conservative to Parliament. Owen Paterson, the former MP and ex-minister who was forced to resign last month after a lobbying scandal, won it with a whopping majority of 23,000 at the last election in 2019. So polls showing that the Conservatives might lose it in a by-election on December 16th have rattled even the most buoyant Tories. The party lost Chesham and Amersham in London’s commuter belt, another formerly safe seat, in June. Some Tories fear that, after weeks of government scandals and U-turns, a good thrashing in Shropshire could be a death blow for Boris Johnson’s leadership of his party—and therefore his tenure in 10 Downing Street. Just how significant are by-election defeats, and can they bring down prime ministers and governments?

Turnout in by-elections is generally low: between 1997 and 2017 it was 41% on average. Party loyalists often use them as a protest vote, safe in the knowledge that the government will not fall. Even if they lose North Shropshire, the Tories will still have a majority of 78 in the House of Commons. All the same, a defeat, with many Conservative voters abstaining or backing the Liberal Democrats, would rank among the most humiliating reversals in British electoral history. The only recent comparison would be with Christchurch, in south-western England, where in 1993 another 23,000 Tory majority was eviscerated by the Liberal Democrats, on a swing of 35%. Ivor Crewe, a prominent electoral analyst, described the result as “off the Richter Scale of by-election earthquakes”.


A look back at past by-election defeats for Conservative governments suggests several political facts. The immediate beneficiary is usually the Liberal Democrats. After the party’s win in Chesham and Amersham Sir Ed Davey, the Lib Dems’ leader, knocked down a wall of blue bricks with an effete orange hammer (his party’s colour). But Liberal Democrats have never benefited from by-election triumphs in the long term. There is little to suggest that a win in North Shropshire denotes anything different. And for Conservatives, big losses have often proved survivable. Optimists in the party will refer to Margaret Thatcher’s record. Her governments regularly lost seats in by-elections (four from 1983 to 1987, on an average swing of -14%) but still won huge majorities in general elections.

Some comfort for Conservatives, perhaps—but beware the fate of John Major. His government’s by-election loss in Christchurch was sandwiched between defeats to the Lib Dems almost as devastating, at Newbury and Eastleigh. Voters sensed weakness and division. The by-election at Dudley West, a Midlands seat, in 1994 was the first after Tony Blair was elected Labour leader. His party won it from the Conservatives on a 29% swing. Those defeats created momentum against the government. Its panicky response didn’t help either. Sir John resigned as Conservative leader, forcing an election to flush out his critics. If he had thereby intended to look strong it didn’t work; his hapless government went down to a landslide defeat two years later. The lesson for Mr Johnson is to respond before voters get a taste for blood—to keep the backing of his fellow Tories, first, and then the electorate in 2023 or 2024. And to weigh the result in North Shropshire carefully.
